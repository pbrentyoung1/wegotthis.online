# Turnaround Time, SLA, and Capacity

## Purpose

Define how ForWorship Creative should handle turnaround time, lead time, service expectations, rush work, intake timing, and capacity awareness.

This document exists to protect communications teams from unsustainable urgency while helping department leaders understand what is realistic, what is possible, and when outside support may be needed.

## Core Principle

Turnaround time is a stewardship issue.

The system should help teams plan wisely, protect priority work, and communicate realistic expectations without making requesters feel scolded or blocked.

The product should say, in effect:

> We want to help you do this well. Here is what this kind of work normally requires, here is our current capacity, and here are the options if the timing is tight.

## Partner, Not Gatekeeper

TAT / SLA guidance should feel like partnership, not bureaucracy.

Avoid:

- Your request violates policy.
- This cannot be done.
- You missed the deadline.
- Submit earlier next time.

Prefer:

- This type of work usually needs two weeks.
- Our team is already committed to priority work during this window.
- We can help you look at options.
- A smaller version may be possible.
- This may need vendor or outsourced support.
- We can defer this or plan it for a later window.

## Definitions

### Turnaround Time (TAT)

Turnaround Time is the normal amount of time needed to complete a type of work well.

Examples:

- Social post: 2 to 4 business days.
- Email: 3 to 5 business days.
- Short video: 2 to 4 weeks.
- Print/signage: 2 to 4 weeks depending on vendor needs.

TAT should include production, review, approval, scheduling, vendor handoff, and reasonable revision time where relevant.

### SLA / Service Level Expectation

SLA is the expectation the communications team sets for how quickly certain types of requests can be reviewed, triaged, started, or completed.

In church communications, this should generally be framed as a service expectation rather than a rigid corporate contract.

### Lead Time

Lead Time is how far in advance a request should be submitted before the desired completion, publish, event, in-hand, or install date.

Lead time may be longer than production time because it includes intake, clarification, triage, scheduling, review, and capacity planning.

### Intake Response Time

Intake Response Time is how quickly Communications expects to acknowledge, review, or triage a submitted request.

Example:

- New requests are reviewed within 1 to 2 business days.
- Rush requests are reviewed as soon as possible, but acceptance depends on capacity and priority.

### Rush Work

Rush work is work requested with less than the normal TAT / lead time.

Rush jobs can occur, but they should be rare.

The system should make rush impact visible without shaming the requester.

### Capacity

Capacity is the team’s available time, attention, and production ability during a given window.

Capacity may be reduced by:

- Priority campaigns.
- Major events.
- Staff availability.
- Holidays.
- Existing project load.
- Vendor turnaround.
- Review bottlenecks.
- Outsourced work already in progress.

## Why This Matters

A department may not know that Communications is fully booked for the next three weeks with a priority project.

The system should surface that reality early so the team can make wise decisions.

Possible outcomes:

- Adjust the scope.
- Move the requested date.
- Use a simpler deliverable.
- Reuse an existing asset.
- Outsource to a vendor.
- Defer the project.
- Escalate for priority review.
- Decline the request in its current timing.

The goal is not to say no faster. The goal is to make the true cost of yes visible.

## Where TAT / SLA Belongs

TAT and SLA should be connected to templates and triage.

### Project Templates

Project Templates should define the default work package and recommended planning window.

Examples:

- Podcast Episode.
- Event Promotion.
- Sermon Series Support.
- Sunday Service Support.
- Video Production Package.

Project Templates may include:

- Recommended intake lead time.
- Default project duration.
- Default project-level milestones.
- Default deliverable lineup.
- Default closeout expectations.
- Capacity assumptions.

### Deliverable Types

Deliverable Types should define the production-specific TAT / SLA expectations.

Examples:

- Video.
- Social Post.
- Email.
- Signage.
- Bulletin Insert.
- Sermon Slide.
- Podcast Audio.
- Show Notes.
- Website Update.

Deliverable Types may include:

- Normal turnaround time.
- Minimum recommended lead time.
- Rush threshold.
- Required inputs before work can begin.
- Typical review time.
- Typical revision window.
- Vendor time if relevant.
- Publish/send/print/install timing.
- Closeout requirements.

## Project Template vs Deliverable Type

Project Templates define the lineup.

Deliverable Types define the production details.

Example:

```text
Project Template: Podcast Episode
  Default deliverables:
    - Episode outline
    - Recorded audio
    - Edited audio
    - Show notes
    - Episode artwork
    - Website episode page
    - Social post
    - Newsletter blurb
```

Each deliverable then uses a Deliverable Type with its own TAT and field requirements.

Example:

```text
Deliverable Type: Social Post
  Normal TAT: 2 to 4 business days
  Rush threshold: less than 2 business days
  Required inputs: message, CTA/link, publish date, platform
```

## Intake Behavior

During intake, the system should collect enough timing information to identify whether the request appears normal, tight, or rushed.

Useful intake questions:

- When does this need to be completed?
- Is there an event date, publish date, send date, print deadline, or install date?
- Is this date flexible?
- What happens if this is not ready by that date?
- Is this tied to an already-approved priority?
- Are there existing assets or copy?
- Do you already have approval from the ministry owner or decision maker?

The requester should not have to know the TAT rules. The system should interpret the dates and help Communications respond.

## Triage Behavior

During triage, the Communications Lead should see:

- Requested due date.
- Event/publish/send/print/install date.
- Recommended TAT based on project template and deliverable types.
- Whether the request is normal, tight, or rushed.
- Current capacity warnings.
- Missing inputs that may delay work.
- Vendor or outsourcing recommendation if timing exceeds internal capacity.
- Scope options.

Example triage language:

> This request includes video and signage. Based on normal turnaround time, this usually needs 3 to 4 weeks. The requested date is 10 business days away, and the team is already committed to Easter priority work during this window. Consider reducing scope, moving the date, or using vendor support.

## Rush Job Behavior

Rush jobs should be possible, but intentionally visible.

A rush request should require Communications to choose one of the following:

- Accept as rush.
- Reduce scope.
- Outsource or vendor-support.
- Defer.
- Decline in current timeline.
- Escalate for priority decision.

Rush acceptance should record:

- Who approved rush handling.
- Why it was accepted.
- What scope changed.
- What other work may be affected.
- Whether vendor/outsourced help is needed.

This protects the team and creates institutional memory.

## Capacity Awareness

The system should help Communications understand whether the requested work fits into the current work window.

MVP capacity can be light.

MVP may use:

- Project start/stop dates.
- Existing active project count.
- Priority flags.
- Blocked work.
- Due-soon deliverables.
- Team member assignments.
- Manual capacity notes.

Post-MVP may add:

- Estimated hours.
- Role-based capacity.
- Team calendars.
- Workload scoring.
- Vendor capacity.
- Department utilization.
- Forecasting.

MVP should not attempt a full resource management engine.

## Capacity Notes

A Communications Lead should be able to add capacity notes for a time window.

Examples:

- Easter production priority through March 31.
- Communications team at reduced capacity June 10-17.
- Video production unavailable next week.
- Print/vendor turnaround currently 10 business days.
- Outsourcing recommended for new video projects this month.

These notes can help triage without building a complex scheduling engine.

## User-Facing Capacity Guidance

Requester-facing messaging should be plain and calm.

Examples:

- This type of work usually needs more lead time.
- The team is already committed to priority work during this window.
- We can help look at a smaller version.
- We may need to use a vendor or outside support.
- We can plan this for a later date.
- Communications will review and suggest the best next step.

Avoid exposing internal workload details that are not helpful or appropriate.

## Priority Work

Priority work should affect capacity guidance.

Priority may be assigned to:

- Campaigns.
- Projects.
- Deliverables.
- Organizational initiatives.

Examples:

- Easter.
- Christmas.
- Capital campaign.
- Emergency communication.
- Executive priority.
- Safety/security communication.

Priority work may reduce available capacity for other work.

The system should surface priority conflicts during triage.

## Vendor / Outsource Recommendation

The system should allow Communications to mark when outside help may be needed.

Reasons:

- Internal team is at capacity.
- Timeline is too short.
- Specialized skill is needed.
- Print/signage/vendor production is required.
- Video workload exceeds internal capacity.
- Project is approved but cannot fit internal schedule.

Vendor recommendation should be visible to Communications and authorized internal roles. It may or may not be visible to requesters depending on workflow.

## Status and Attention States

TAT / SLA and capacity should inform attention states.

Examples:

- Normal timing.
- Tight timing.
- Rush request.
- Capacity conflict.
- Vendor support may be needed.
- Waiting on required input.
- At risk.
- Date may need adjustment.

These should be plain-language indicators, not punitive warnings.

## Data Concepts

Potential data concepts:

- Project Template TAT defaults.
- Deliverable Type TAT defaults.
- Lead time requirements.
- Rush thresholds.
- Intake response target.
- Review duration estimates.
- Revision duration estimates.
- Vendor duration estimates.
- Capacity notes.
- Priority windows.
- Rush approval record.
- Scope adjustment record.
- Outsource/vendor recommendation.

## MVP Recommendation

For MVP:

- Document default TAT / SLA expectations by Project Template and Deliverable Type.
- Add requested due date, project start date, project stop date, and deliverable due date fields.
- Add simple normal/tight/rush indicators during triage.
- Add manual capacity notes.
- Add priority flags.
- Add vendor/outsource recommendation field.
- Add rush approval notes.
- Avoid building a full capacity planning engine.

## Future Expansion

Future versions may add:

- Admin-configurable TAT/SLA rules.
- Admin-configurable Project Templates.
- Admin-configurable Deliverable Types.
- Recurring production capacity planning.
- Automated date calculations.
- Lead-time warnings.
- Workload forecasting.
- Role-based capacity.
- Vendor capacity tracking.
- SLA reporting.
- Department utilization reporting.
