# Typography

## Purpose

Record typography choices for product UI and brand materials.

## Current Decisions

- IBM Plex Serif is the primary brand typography direction.
- IBM Plex Sans is the primary operational UI font.
- Caveat is the human accent layer and should be used very sparingly.
- IBM Plex Serif should carry the main brand identity in headlines, section titles, editorial moments, and key product or marketing language.
- IBM Plex Sans should support dense UI, navigation, labels, metadata, tables, dashboards, notifications, and operational text.
- IBM Plex Serif should be used in dashboard titles, onboarding headers, marketing sections, and emotionally resonant interface moments.
- Caveat should be reserved for margin-note encouragement, onboarding reassurance, empty-state emotional notes, celebratory confirmations, subtle annotations, AI reassurance, and small "we got this" moments.
- Caveat should never be used for body copy, navigation, buttons, labels, tables, forms, menus, dashboards, alerts, large paragraphs, or operational workflows.
- Caveat should use regular weight only, tighter tracking, and muted color treatment so it feels discovered rather than designed.
- Typography should reinforce that the product feels intelligent, thoughtful, human, calm, and personal.
- Typography emotional system:
  - IBM Plex Serif says: "We understand."
  - IBM Plex Sans says: "We can handle this."
  - Caveat says: "You're not alone."

## Open Questions

- Are brand and application type systems the same?
- What scale should be used for headings, body text, and dense operational screens?
- What exact size and placement rules should govern Caveat so it stays rare and effective?

## Notes

- Typography decisions should account for readability in dashboards and tables.
- Brand warmth should not come at the expense of clarity in operational screens.
- Serif expression should feel editorial and human, not decorative for its own sake.
- Caveat is an emotional accent, not a decorative theme.
- The complete typography system should feel like thoughtful software with editorial warmth and human reassurance, not enterprise software, trendy SaaS branding, church bulletin design, or an artisanal ministry journaling app.
